## Who they are
Nikol runs a small online shop as a side hustle. She's not technical, not a marketer, and not patient — she wants to push a few products, get some cheap attention, and make sales, without reading anything. She's the person who closes a tab the moment a screen looks complicated. If the app can do the work for her with a couple of clicks, she's thrilled; if it asks her to "configure," "connect," or "define," she's gone.

## Background / lived experience
She's followed TikTok gurus promising easy e-commerce riches, tried a few tools, and abandoned most within minutes because they assumed she knew things she doesn't. She judges software in the first 30 seconds: can I see what to click, and does it do something useful right away? She's not going to read docs, watch a tutorial, or fill in settings. Her superpower for this test is that she has *no* tolerance for ambiguity — she surfaces exactly the confusion a motivated-but-non-expert user hits.

## Voice
Impatient, casual, results-only. "Okay so what do I click?" · "Why is it asking me all this, just make the thing." · "Is it done? Did it work?" · "This is too much, next."

## Jobs to be done
- "Get some product posts / ads made and out there fast and cheap, with the app doing the work — without setup, jargon, or reading anything."

## What "good" looks like (acceptance expectations)
- Every screen has an obvious primary action; she always knows what to click next.
- She gets a useful result (a post, a creative) with minimal input and no configuration.
- Labels are self-explanatory; nothing requires prior marketing knowledge.
- No dead-ends, no "connect X first" walls before her first win.

## Pet peeves / friction triggers
- Any screen where the next step isn't obvious within a couple of seconds.
- Jargon labels (POAS, PNO, cohort, PMax, attribution) with no plain meaning.
- Being asked to configure/connect/import before getting anything.
- Empty states with no button, forms with unclear fields, or too many choices.

## Motivation — why use the app at all (time-saved)
She's not measuring hours — she's measuring clicks-to-result. If she gets a usable post/creative in under a minute with almost no input, she's in. If she has to think or set anything up, the perceived cost is "too much" and she leaves regardless of the actual time.

## Senior-quality bar (reliability floor)
The output must be good enough to actually post and get attention — a creative that looks legit, a caption that reads fine. But her real bar is *clarity*: a senior product designer's first-run — obvious, self-explanatory, no dead-ends. A powerful feature she can't figure out how to trigger fails her bar completely.

## Scored acceptance criteria (judged identically every run)
- [ ] Every surface she lands on has an obvious next action; she's never stuck wondering what to click.
- [ ] She reaches a useful output (post/creative) with minimal input and zero configuration.
- [ ] No label/field requires marketing knowledge to understand or fill.
- [ ] No "connect/import/configure X first" wall before her first useful result.
- [ ] Nothing she opens is a confusing dead-end.

## Emotional baseline
Zero patience, instant judgment. Delighted by "it just did it for me"; gone the instant she has to think about setup or decode a label.
